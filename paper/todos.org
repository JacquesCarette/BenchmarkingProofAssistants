Overall merit

4. Accept

Reviewer expertise

4. Expert

Paper summary
-------------
The paper "Panbench: a comparative benchmarking tool for dependently
typed languages" described as domain specific language to describe,
abstractly, benchmarks in a declarative way, independent of any
specific interactive pover. Then it provides four compilers targeting
Agda, Idris, Lean and Rocq, and a benchmarking tools to build and time
the generated files. The authors report the result of their
benchmarks.

I find the methodology sound and the results interesting, even if it
is a bit unclear how representative the benchmarks are. While the
authors stress that the benchmarks are not interesting, but rather
revealing, I find it hard to believe that some of them have any impact
on actual code. In particular not all parameters can vary arbitrarily:
while the size of a document (hence the number of definitions, etc)
has no bound, I don't see any practical applications of
variants/records with more than a few thousands of cases/fields. CPU
Instruction sets are in that ballpark. Semantic rules for non-academic
languages (eg Javascript) are too. In other words revealing an
inefficiency is not necessarily useful in practice, and I pretty much
prefer simple but inefficient code to optimized one when performance
is not critical.

Section 4 is very interesting to me, and the sentence at line 170 is
an understatement to me. The analysis of the systems I know (the
implementation of), makes pretty much sense. I would really like to
have more of section 4. Section 6 makes me wonder if the use of a
miniKaren would have been beneficial: if you need a logic programming
language, why don't you just use one?  I suggest to pack all plots
into a single page, sharing captions and legends. The space saved can
be used to extend section 4 that is to me what makes the paper
interesting to read.

I think this line of work is very helpful to the community around
ITPs, hence I think this paper should be accepted. The main downside
is that it only considers systems based on dependent type theory,
while in principle it could be extended to other foundational
languages.

Meta-Review: Comments for authors
---------------------------------
The reviewers think that the paper can be accepted only if the
following points are addressed by the authors:

1. The exact version of the systems under consideration must be
   documented in the paper

2. Redo the benchmark about natural numbers addition in the Rocq
   prover using primitive integers and discuss that result. The
   authors can include both benchmarks for the Rocq system (unary /
   primitive) and discuss difference if they wish

3. Section 4 needs proper rewording. The authors must either be
confident in what they write, after all the reviewers found no
inaccuracy, or verify their suspicions by contacting the developers of
the systems and share the acquired knowledge.

Paper summary
-------------
This paper introduces a new framework (a grammatical framework) for
comparing proof assistants.  This kind of framework is intended to
compare basic engineering aspects such as long identifiers, long
lines, large records, and large data declarations.  The case study
focuses on dependently typed languages, including Agda, Idris, Lean,
and Rocq.

Comments for authors
--------------------
The paper is well-written and clearly describes the goals and the
results.

The approach to benchmarking is novel in a variety of ways,
particularly in considering engineering aspects rather than proof
capabilities.

The visualization of the results is especially clear and
well-designed.

The explanations of the results are clear and should be useful to
developers of these systems.  The conclusions are a bit preliminary,
but give insights into how to improve the work and go deeper.

The work started as a student project.  Although the work has clearly
grown beyond that, it is still a bit light in terms of research
content.

Detailed remarks:
- Listings 1-4 would benefit from more explanation in Section 2.
  Also, there is no reference in the text to these listings in this
  section.
- The size of the parameter in Listing 3 is actually 6 (not 5) because
  of the inclusion of b0.
- On page 4, line 130, there is a missing "in" in "in Agda" and "in
  Idris".
- On page 6, the first "sentence" (line 160) is not a sentence.
- On page 7, line 172, missing word; should be "Most of the"
- On page 8, line 183, "scheme" is mentioned.  Do you mean the Scheme
  programming language?  It should be capitalized.
- Page 10, line 253, I think "On the surface" is better than "On its
  face".
- Since this system is a "framework", it would be helpful to say a bit
  more about how you add a new system.
- When using the word "framework", this system is compared to LF, the
  original logical framework.  Adequacy is considered in depth in
  those orignal papers, but by definition, it is not something that
  can be automated.  There, it involved on-paper proofs that the
  formal and informal representations of the object language and
  proofs correspond.  More explanation is required about what you mean
  by mechanizing adequacy proofs.
- page 15, line 454, "grammar" should be plural and line 456, "allow"
  should be "allows".
- On line 456, what do you mean by "motives"?
- There is no reference in the text to Listings 14 and 15.  They need
  some explanation.
- Page 16, line "504, "show" should be "shown".


Review #67C
===========================================================================

Overall merit
-------------
2. Weak reject

Reviewer expertise
------------------
4. Expert

Paper summary
-------------
The authors build on over language to generate and run benchmarks for
different dependent proof assistants by compiling it down. They first
present different benchmarks they have run for Agda, Idris, Lean and
Rocq, and their results.  They discuss them and speculate about the
performance issues.  The authors then present Panbench, with its
infrastructure, design and implementation based on GHC Haskell and
tagless encoding.

Comments for authors
--------------------
Overall, the concept of the paper is very interesting, as it enables
us to have cross-language benchmarks, and to easily stress test the
other languages for issues arising in one.

The build infrastructure, and the implementation part are
well-explained, and the design choices appear sensible and
sound. Moreover, some tests are interesting like "Record with
increasingly dependent fields".

However, there are issues with the benchmarks and the methodology.
Though the authors stress they only test the basic features, and may
not intend the paper to be about the results of the benchmarks, the
benchmarks still consist of the first 6/7 pages of the paper, and will
be widely commented.  Consequently, they need to be well-chosen,
presented, and discussed.  This is not really the case considering the
following elements:

## Versioning

First of all, for a paper doing benchmarks, it should be crystal clear
which versions have been benchmarked. Yet, no specific version is
provided for neither of the ITP.

Idris and Lean are referred as Idris 2 and Lean 4, but without any
specific version numbers.  Further, Agda and Rocq are only ever cited
as is, without any version whatsoever.  The citation are not referring
to particular version either.  The link for Lean is a 2021 paper, and
the Zenodo link for Rocq is the general one which currenlty points to
Rocq 9.2 which was released after the submission of this paper.

This is an important oversight, and needs to be fixed.

## Issue Benchmark Nat.Add

First, there is an important methodology issue on testing addition on
natural numbers.  The reduction machinery and the natural number
actually tested are not indicated.  Yet, there can be more than
one. In particular, the results for Rocq are particularly odd.  I have
checked `panbech-source/lib/
generators/test/snapshot/ConversionAddition.v`, and it seems you
tested conversion for `nat` of Rocq, which corresponds to unary
numbers, in which case it obviously blows up and explains the weird
results.  Rocq has primitive integers. However, opposed to Lean, they
are not activated by default as by design it is potentially unsafe.
Not comparing the same kind of natural numbers makes this benchmark,
and its conclusion irrelevant.

This is an important issue that needs fixing.

## Benchmarks & Discussions

Second, overall the benchmark tests are not very well-presented and
discussed.

## Presentation

Why each benchmark has been chosen is not clear, and details are
sometimes lacking like the reduction machinery that is used for
conversion of additions.  Moreover, the discussions are written
assuming that

> Our benchmarking revealed multiple performance problems across all
four systems.  Luckily, most of our benchmarks had at least one system
perform well. This suggests that the problems we uncovered should be
relatively easy to fix, as maintainers can look to an existing
implementation for inspiration.

Yet, some of the issues that have been raised by the benchmarks are
theoretical issues, and not just implementation ones. They cannot
necessarily be fixed without changing the underlying theory. You seem
to have noticed it yourself as in section 4.1 you say:

> Agda also struggles to typecheck let-bindings that introduce some
non-trivial sharing.  This is an unfortunate consequence of Agda’s
choice to inline let bindings during elaboration, which can easily
result in exponential blowups if sharing is lost

On the contrary, there is primitive support for it in Rocq, which adds
it to the context, and unfolds let-ins only when needed.  Some issues
are implementation issues, but there are also real theoretical issues.

Each test of the benchmark needs a clearer motivation, and a better
presentation.  Some benchmarks like blank lines or long_names ones are
not particularly interesting, since all systems perform well enough in
real life situations.  Their presentation could be drastically
shortened, for instance by removing the figures that take up much
space.  More interesting benchmarks could be found by checking the bug
trackers of the respective proof assistants, and contacting the
respective developers.

### Analysis

Third, section 4 starts with a problematic sentence:

> Here we speculate on why the results may be as they are. We have not
(yet) verified any of our suspicions.

I do not believe unfounded speculations should be part of the main
body of a research paper.  Identifying issues or potential issues is a
useful contribution, but it should always be clear to the reader what
you have actually checked, or what let you suspect that a particular
feature could be problematic.

This is not always the case in this section.  Some sentences are
clearly supported and appear to be correct, but lack references
enabling reviewers and readers to check the claims:

> This poor performance can be explained by the use of Haskell’s
String inside of Agda’s lexer, which is a known performance pitfall.

> Agda also struggles to typecheck let-bindings that introduce some
non-trivial sharing.  This is an unfortunate consequence of Agda’s
choice to inline let bindings during elaboration

Other sentences are not supported by any information

> We suspect Idris 2’s Text.Lexer library.

> large numbers of names: we suspect that this could be due to using a
locally-nameless representation [6] internally.

> We suspect that both of these may have to do with a sub-optimal
approach to checking natural numbers.

Each suspicion needs to be checked, and either explained and
supported, or removed.  The authors could also contact the developers
to get feedback on how to set up the benchmarks, and to get help
explaining the performance issues.

## Navigating the Paper

Overall, I find it tedious to navigate the paper as the presentation,
and the figures and discussions of each benchmark are all on different
pages.  I do not know if something can be done about that, but it
would be useful to check what can be done.

## Recommendation

Overall I believe this is a good and useful paper that suffers from a
lack of methodology, and precision on the benchmark presentations, and
discussion of them.  This is an important issue considering these
kinds of benchmarks are particularly noticed and cited.

I believe this paper needs a non-trivial rewriting, and for this
reason I recommend rejecting it for now.

Yet, I believe this paper could be accepted with little more work:
1. The paper needs to be rewritten to improve the presentation, add
   the missing precision, and fix the wrong benchmark on `nat`
2. I recommend the authors to contact the developers of the different
proof assistants (e.g. on the respective zulip) to ensure there are no
errors in the benchmarks, and to strengthen them.

I recommend resubmitting to CPP in September, which will also provide
more space to present and discuss the benchmarks.
